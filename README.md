Do HR metrics just show past numbers, or can they act as an early warning system to prevent employee burnout and costly turnover? 🤔📊

I am thrilled to share my latest project: IBM HR Analytics - Employee Attrition & Performance Dashboard, which I built entirely from scratch using Power BI.

My goal was to conduct a "digital forensic investigation" into human resources data to connect hidden variables, uncover the true Root Causes behind employee turnover, and deliver actionable strategic recommendations for leadership teams.

📉 Page 1: Overview
Objective: Establish the fundamental baseline structure of the company's workforce.

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):

Total Employees (1470): Reflects the overall workforce size to provide clear data context.

Average Monthly Income ($6,503): Sets the baseline salary for employees across the company.

Overtime Rate (28.30%): Highlights the percentage of employees facing overtime pressure company-wide.

Average Performance (3.15): Measures overall employee productivity based on standard metrics.

Attrition Rate (16.12%): The annual baseline turnover that leadership aims to minimize.

Key Analysis: This page establishes the demographic and baseline visual foundation (by age, gender, and job roles) to compare against when identifying anomalies.

📉 Page 2: Attrition Analysis
Objective: Deep dive into the precise behaviors of the 237 employees who actually left the company to identify turnover drivers.

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):

Total Attrition (237): The exact headcount of talent lost.

Average Age of Attrition (34 years old): Indicates that young and mid-career professionals—the peak productivity demographic—are leaving the most.

Average Income of Attrition ($4,787): A critical warning sign proving that those who left earned roughly 26% less than the company average ($6,503).

Distance Attrition (10.63 KM): Quantitatively proves that longer commutes serve as a logistical barrier that accelerates resignation.

Key Analysis: The data reveals that the Research & Development (R&D) department experienced the highest turnover with 133 cases, specifically concentrated in the Laboratory Technician role with 62 resignations.
Furthermore, the majority of departing employees suffered from a combination of workplace and logistical pressure: over half worked Overtime while commuting distances exceeding 10 KM daily, making physical and logistical exhaustion a primary driver for leaving, particularly in R&D and Sales.

🔍 Strategic Insights

1️⃣ The Burnout Trap:
73 employees resigned despite reporting high job satisfaction (Level 3)! Data shows 57.3% of this group faced extreme burnout due to a heavy combination of Overtime, long commutes exceeding 10 KM, and low average monthly incomes ($4,596). Psychological satisfaction cannot shield talent from physical exhaustion.

2️⃣ The Commute Barrier:
The stabilization of the average commute distance at 10.63 KM for departing employees highlights a critical geographic threshold. Exceeding this boundary accelerates turnover, signaling an urgent need for flexible corporate solutions, such as online/remote work models.


3️⃣ Early Attrition:
Turnover is highly concentrated within the first 24 months of tenure, severely impacting Laboratory Technicians (62 resignations) and Sales Executives (57 resignations) due to high initial stress, meaning the company rapidly loses its investment in training new hires.# IBM_HR_Analytics_Dashboard
